During my Master's thesis, I collected data depicting the dispersal of individual snails from three different points of release over the course of five days, which ended up being 9 low tides after release. For two of the locations, the distance dispersed could be modeled by probability density functions but for one location, the spread of individual snails from the first tide after release onwards looked multimodal and could not be effectively modeled with a simple probability density function. Below I outline my process of modelling some of this data with kernel density estimation.

I imported all the packages that we would need and then the data. After checking that the data imported successfully, I lightly cleaned it by removing the first two columns. The column 'location' held the information for which one of the three locations these snails were released at, and the column '0' showed the distance from the release point each snail was at the 0th tide (the release day). The other columns are the distance in metres at 1m intervals (up to 10m) from the release point after a specific number of low tides (e.g. column '5' is the distance in metres from the release point five low tides after release).

# Function that takes as input a list of data and the number of bins
# Returns the best fit distribution, its parameters, and its sum of squared estimate of errors (SSE)
# The best fit distribution is determined based on the lowest SSE
# Tries all continuous distributions in scipy.stats
def get_best_distribution(data, bins=50):
    data = data.dropna()
    y, x = np.histogram(data, bins=bins, density=True)
    x = np.linspace(0,10,50)

    # Best holders
    best_distributions = []
    
    with warnings.catch_warnings():
        warnings.simplefilter("ignore")
        for index, distribution in enumerate([d for d in _distn_names if not d in ['levy_stable', 'studentized_range']]):
            print("{:>3} / {:<3}: {}".format(index+1, len(_distn_names), distribution))
            distribution = getattr(st, distribution)

            try:
                params = distribution.fit(data)
                arg = params[:-2]
                loc = params[-2]
                scale = params[-1]

                pdf = distribution.pdf(x, loc=loc, scale=scale, *arg)
                sse = np.sum(np.power(y - pdf, 2.0))

                best_distributions.append([distribution, params, sse])

            except Exception:
                pass

    best = sorted(best_distributions, key=lambda x:x[1])
    best = best[0]
    
    return best

# Function that takes in data, grid used for plotting, and bandwidth
# Returns the values at each point for the kde
def kde_scipy(x, x_grid, bandwidth=0.2, **kwargs):
    kde = gaussian_kde(x, bw_method=bandwidth, **kwargs)
    return kde.evaluate(x_grid)

The kernel density estimation calculation uses a bandwidth and different bandwidths can lead to vastly different results, as seen in the graph below.

Given that varying the bandwidth between 0.1 and 1.0 can produce such different results and that I do not have any references for what the best bandwidth for this data would be, I use cross-validation to find the best bandwidth. In cross validation, the model is fit to part of the data, and then a quantitative metric is computed to determine how well this model fits the remaining data. This should then provide the best bandwidth fit for our estimation.

Cross-validation shows that the best bandwidth to use is 0.1m. This does give the most exact fit to the data, as seen when I plotted the kernel density estimations using bandwidths of 0.1, 0.3, and 1.0. What can also be seen is that the lower the bandwidth, the more exact the line plotted from the estimation follows the data but the higher the bandwidth, the smoother the plotted line is.

In this situation, the data collected was in 1 metre increments due to natural constraints of what data is feasible to collect in the field. We would assume that the areas between each metre increment would follow a smooth distribution. Therefore, we use a bandwidth slightly larger than 0.1 to smooth out the areas where we have missing data but do not lose the bimodal shape.

This process can be repeated with the data collected from after different numbers of tides or for the data as a whole.
